{{GW|ODOS}} is an important word in Mark.  It occurs mostly in certain places, highlighting structural boundaries.  The exceptions is Mark 2:23, which is anarthrous and seems to be unique and free-standing.

Mark 1:2,3 are part of a quote.  John the Baptist self-consciously prepares Yahweh's way (τὴν ὁδὸν Κυρίου).  We are prepared by 1:1 in a wayJohn is not, so the scene by the Jordan is ironic.

Mark 4:4, 15 describe the beleaguered plant near the road (παρὰ τὴν ὁδὸν).  10:46 describes Blind Bartimeaus in the exact same phrase: not a coincidence.  Jesus rescues him from the clutches of Satan.

Mark 6:8 is part of Jesus instructions to the disciples regarding their missionary journey.  They are to take nothing "on the road" or "into the way" (εἰς ὁδὸν).  When Jesus meets a man of means, Mark 10:17, the same exact phrase applies to Jesus' journey.  He does not show partiality towards the rich.

The key use of ὁδὸς occurs six time in the phrase "ἐν τῇ ὁδῷ" - 8:3, 27, 9:33, 34, 10:32 and 52.  It seem equivalent to the modern, evangelicalism "walking the Christian Life".  The 4000 Gentiles who are about to be miraculously fed in 8:3 are *going to be* "in the way" - it is yet future for them.  Otherwise, all these references are about the disciples and Jesus in the center section of Mark, 8:22—10:52.  This section is a set of three passion predictions and misunderstandings, bracketed by an *inclusio* of the two-part healing and the immediate healing of Bartimeaus.  As already noted, he is at first described as "beside the road" (παρὰ τὴν ὁδὸν) but Jesus brings him ἐν τῇ ὁδῷ.

The fickle mob, which would soon enough cry "crucify!", places clothes and branches "upon the way" (εἰς τὴν ὁδόν) - Mark 11:8.  Throughout Mark, the mob is an impediment the truly faithful.  Here, as always before, they ape the disciples (cp. 11:7).  They do homage, but it is merely "on top" of the Christian walk, not "in" it.

Lastly, there is the doubly ironic scene with Pharisees and the Herodians (12:14).  They are insincere when they praise Jesus for teaching "the way of God" (τὴν ὁδὸν τοῦ Θεοῦ). We and John Mark's original audience appreciate the dramatic irony that He actually does just that.  Quite the opposite of John the Baptism, the make crooked the straight and murder the Son of God.
