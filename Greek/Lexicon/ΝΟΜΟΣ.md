Cranfield ... notes the fact that the Greek of Paul's day had no words to represent the ideas of "legalism," "legalist" and "legalistic."
<blockquote>
This means not just that he did not have a convenient terminology to express a key idea, but that he had no definite, ready-made concept of legalism with which to work in his own mind.  And this means, surely, that he was at a very considerable disadvantage  compared with the modern theologian, when he had to attempt to clarify the Christian position with regard to the law.  In view of this, we should, I think, be ready to reckon with the possibility that sometimes, when he appears to be disparaging the law, what he really has in mind may not be the law itself but the misunderstanding and misuse of it for which *we* have a convenient term.<ref>Cranfield, *St. Paul and the Law*, 55</ref>
</blockquote>
