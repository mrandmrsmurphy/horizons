This word *euangellion* has a rich heritage before the NT and is linked to key concepts in Judaism and Roman culture.  In Rome, the term was connect with the imperial cult.  "Its 'good news' was that Caesar, the son of God, was now the lord of the whole world, claiming allegiance from everybody in return for bringing salvation and justice to the world.  Resistance was met with crucifixion." <ref>N.T. Wright, Bible Review, December 2000</ref>  In the Hebrew Bible, the word בְּשׂוֹרָה/bĕsowrah was used specifically of "good news".  It was translated by the producers of the LXX once with *euangellion* and five times with its feminine cousin εὐαγγελία.  The verbs εὐαγγελίζω is used 20 times to translate בָּשַׂר/basar.  Almost all the uses are to proclaim a military victory.

In the Vulgate, the 6 OT usages of *euangel-* come across via nuntius, just plain 'news'.  However, all 86 NT uses of εὐαγγέλιον are translated with evangelium.  We have followed suit, using "good news" in all place of the verb or noun(s) in English and בְּשׂוֹרָה/bĕsowrah or בָּשַׂר/basar in Hebrew.
